# Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love

- **Author:** Marty Cagan (Silicon Valley Product Group)
- **Editions:** 1st (2008), 2nd (2017)
- **Field:** Product management — how strong product teams operate

## Thesis
Strong product organizations ship outcomes, not features. They run
discovery and delivery as parallel loops, treat product management as a
craft (not a service role), and give empowered teams problems to solve
rather than features to build.

## Core ideas
- **Outcomes over outputs.** Roadmaps full of features are a misuse;
  bets and outcomes are the unit of work. Ask "what change in user
  behavior or business metric proves this worked?"
- **Discovery vs. delivery.** Discovery answers *should* we build this.
  Delivery answers *can* we build it well. Run them in parallel, not in
  sequence.
- **The four big product risks.**
  1. **Value** — will customers buy / use it?
  2. **Usability** — can they figure out how?
  3. **Feasibility** — can engineering build it within constraints?
  4. **Business viability** — does it work for our business (legal,
     finance, sales, support)?
  Discovery's job is to retire all four risks before delivery starts.
- **Empowered teams.** Small cross-functional teams (product, design,
  engineering) given problems and outcomes — not specifications.
- **Product manager ≠ product owner ≠ project manager.** PM owns value
  + viability discovery; designer owns usability; tech lead owns
  feasibility. Owner / scrum-master roles are coordination, not the job.
- **Prototypes as the discovery instrument.** Cheap, fast, used to test
  one risk at a time. A prototype is for learning, not shipping.
- **Customer interviews and customer discovery program.** Continuous,
  not quarterly.
- **Vision, strategy, principles** ahead of feature lists. Without these
  three, every feature debate is a re-litigation.

## Named frameworks & heuristics
- **Four product risks** (value / usability / feasibility / viability).
- **Dual-track agile** (discovery and delivery as parallel tracks).
- **Empowered product teams.**

## How to apply
- Replace feature lists with **problem statements + outcome metrics.**
- For every initiative, name which of the four risks is the riskiest and
  test it first with the cheapest possible prototype.
- Move PMs out of project-management work; their primary job is
  discovery.
- Stop committing to multi-quarter feature roadmaps; commit to outcomes.

## When to reach for it
Every PM, founder, or engineering lead working with PMs should read it
once. The second edition is the one to buy.

## Caveats
- Aspirational. Assumes organizational buy-in for empowered teams; many
  companies aren't structured to support it.
- Light on metrics frameworks (pair with *Measure What Matters* or
  *Lean Analytics*) and on discovery practice (pair with
  *Continuous Discovery Habits*).
- Repetitive in places — short chapters, same theme.
