# 100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People

- **Author:** Susan Weinschenk, PhD
- **Editions:** 1st (2011), 2nd (2020)
- **Field:** Applied cognitive psychology for design

## Thesis
Cognitive science has findings that translate directly into design
decisions. Each "thing" is a named fact about how human perception,
attention, memory, decision-making, or motivation works — paired with a
practical implication.

## Core ideas (representative; 100 in the book)
- **Peripheral vision is doing more work than designers think.**
  Important signals must enter the periphery; central focus is narrow.
- **People read on screens ~25% slower than on paper.** Don't pack the
  same text density.
- **Mental shortcuts dominate** (System 1). Designs that align with the
  shortcut succeed; ones that fight it leak users.
- **Progress is motivating** (Zeigarnik effect). Unfinished tasks pull;
  visible progress retains.
- **Scarcity and social proof shape choices.** Use them honestly; dark
  patterns burn trust.
- **Working memory holds ~4 chunks.** Wizards, navigation, and forms
  must respect that ceiling.
- **People remember the beginning and end** (serial-position effect).
  Sequence accordingly.
- **Mirror neurons mean stories and faces engage attention** more than
  abstract copy.
- **Attention is captured by motion and novelty** — useful, also
  dangerous (chrome that animates pulls attention from content).
- **Recognition beats recall.** Show, don't ask the user to remember.
- **Defaults matter more than choice architecture.** Most users don't
  change them.
- **Goal-gradient effect** — closer to a goal, the harder users push.
  Show closeness.

## Named frameworks & heuristics
The book does not introduce a single framework — it's structured as 100
labeled patterns each in 2–4 pages, with a research citation and an
implication.

## How to apply
- Treat the book as a **reference / checklist**, not a cover-to-cover
  read.
- Skim the table of contents when starting a new feature; mark items
  relevant to the current problem; read those entries in depth.
- When you find a behavior in a design review you cannot explain, search
  here first; usually the explanation is one of the 100.

## When to reach for it
Throughout your career — keep on the desk. Especially useful for
designers/engineers who lack a cognitive psychology background.

## Caveats
- Each entry is necessarily shallow. For research depth, follow the
  citations.
- A few items reflect older research interpretations; treat the 2020
  edition as the current one.
- The framing is consumer / marketing-friendly; B2B and operator
  interfaces require some translation.
