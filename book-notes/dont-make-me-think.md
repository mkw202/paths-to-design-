# Don't Make Me Think

- **Author:** Steve Krug
- **Editions:** 1st (2000), 2nd (2006), 3rd "Revisited" (2014, adds mobile)
- **Field:** Web usability

## Thesis
Usability is about reducing cognitive load. Users do not read pages — they
scan them, satisfice rather than optimize, and muddle through interfaces.
Design accordingly: every needless question, every moment of ambiguity, is
friction.

## Core ideas
- **Self-evident over self-explanatory.** A page should not need
  instructions; the function should be obvious at a glance.
- **Scanning beats reading.** Users skim for trigger words tied to their
  current task. Format pages for scanning: clear hierarchy, conventions,
  scannable chunks, obvious clickability.
- **Satisficing.** People pick the first acceptable option, not the best.
  Optimize for "good enough first hit," not exhaustive trees.
- **Muddling through.** Users don't figure out how things work; they form
  rough mental models that are often wrong. Design forgives this.
- **Eliminate questions; don't just answer them.** Every "Wait, what do I
  click here?" is a tax. Remove the question by design.
- **Conventions are powerful — break them only when payoff is large.**
  Familiarity has compounding value.
- **Don't waste my goodwill.** Every interaction either adds or subtracts
  from user patience.
- **Usability testing is cheap, mandatory, and works at small N.** Three
  users find the big problems. The "we need 30 users" myth blocks teams
  from testing at all.

## Named frameworks & heuristics
- **The trunk test** — could a stranger figure out, in 5 seconds, what
  site this is, what page they're on, and what to do next?
- **The 5-second test** — show a page for 5 seconds, then ask "what was
  it about?" If they can't answer, the hierarchy is wrong.
- **The "reservoir of goodwill"** — users start trusting; design choices
  either deposit or withdraw.

## How to apply
- Run a 5-user test on anything important before shipping. Lo-fi works.
- Audit screens for questions you're asking the user that you could
  answer for them.
- Make clickable things look clickable. Make non-clickable things look
  non-clickable. Visual ambiguity is a usability bug.
- Cut homepage / dashboard copy in half. Then in half again.

## When to reach for it
First UI book to read in your career. Also the right book to hand a
non-designer collaborator. Two-hour read.

## Caveats
- Web-centric; some examples (frames, splash pages) are historical.
- Light on visual craft — pair with *Refactoring UI*.
- Light on product/strategy — pair with *Inspired*.
