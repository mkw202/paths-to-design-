# Shape Up: Stop Running in Circles and Ship Work That Matters

- **Author:** Ryan Singer (Basecamp / 37signals)
- **Edition:** 2019 (free at basecamp.com/shapeup)
- **Field:** Product development process / project management

## Thesis
Six-week cycles with shaped, bounded work and small autonomous teams
ship more meaningful product than continuous sprints with open backlogs.
The discipline is in **shaping** the work before betting on it, and
**hammering scope** — not the deadline — when reality intervenes.

## Core ideas
- **Six-week cycles + two-week cooldown.** Six is long enough to ship
  something real; cooldown is for fixes, exploration, and shaping next
  cycle's bets.
- **Shaping is upstream.** Senior product people define a pitch:
  problem, appetite (time budget — small batch ~2 weeks, big batch ~6
  weeks), solution sketch at the right resolution (vague where details
  don't matter; specific where they do), rabbit holes called out
  explicitly, and no-gos.
- **Appetite, not estimate.** "How much is this worth?" not "how long
  will it take?" The appetite caps scope; scope adapts to the appetite.
- **Betting table.** A small group (usually founders / senior PM /
  senior eng) reviews shaped pitches and bets on a cycle's worth of
  work. No backlog. Pitches not bet on go back to the shaping pile or
  die.
- **No backlogs.** The backlog is rotting work in disguise. If a pitch
  is still relevant next cycle, someone will re-pitch it.
- **Hill chart over burndown.** A "hill" with an uphill (figuring it
  out) and downhill (executing) side communicates real status better
  than percentage-done.
- **Scope hammering.** Cut scope to hit the appetite — never extend the
  deadline. The team is empowered (and expected) to do this.
- **Small autonomous teams.** Typically one designer + one or two
  engineers. The team owns end-to-end execution within the bet.
- **Bets are commitments inside the cycle, not outside.** Once a team
  is on a bet, they're not interrupted; if a bet fails, that's the bet.

## Named frameworks & heuristics
- **Shaping** as a distinct upstream activity.
- **Appetite** as the project constraint.
- **Betting table** replacing sprint planning.
- **Hill chart.**
- **Scope hammering.**
- **Six-week cycle + two-week cooldown.**

## How to apply
- Even if you don't adopt the full cycle, the **shaping** discipline is
  portable: write pitches with explicit appetite, problem, solution at
  the right resolution, rabbit holes, and no-gos.
- Replace "estimate" conversations with "appetite" conversations: ask
  what the work is worth before asking how long it takes.
- Switch one project's status reporting from burndown to a hill chart
  and see what conversations it surfaces.
- Stop maintaining a backlog. Track ideas, not commitments.

## When to reach for it
When your team feels addicted to sprints / backlogs and isn't shipping
meaningful chunks of work. Also when you're a small team founder
designing a process from scratch — Shape Up is built for that scale.

## Caveats
- Very Basecamp-specific in places; their team size, culture, and
  product (mature, low-velocity-of-change SaaS) shape the prescriptions.
- "No backlog" and "no roadmap" require organizational courage and
  customer-facing tradeoffs that not every business can absorb.
- Light on cross-team coordination — works best when one team owns one
  problem; harder in dependency-heavy environments.
- Free online, which is a feature: skim before deciding to adopt
  anything.
